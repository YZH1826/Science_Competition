Which method of water purification is most effective at removing microbial and chemical contaminants from lakeside water?

  • Different water purification methods will exhibit varying levels of effectiveness in removing contaminants from lakeside water.

Background Research

Access to clean drinking water is a fundamental human right; however, a significant portion of the global population still lacks this essential resource. The World Health Organization reports that approximately 2.3 billion people worldwide do not have access to safe drinking water, which poses various health risks and can even result in fatalities. One of the primary challenges in providing safe drinking water is eliminating contaminants such as bacteria, viruses, and chemical pollutants. Therefore, it is crucial to develop effective water purification methods to ensure the availability of clean and safe drinking water.

  1. Boiling

One traditional method of water purification is boiling, which has been used for centuries. Boiling water kills most microorganisms, making it safe to consume. However, this process does not eliminate chemical contaminants like heavy metals or pesticides. Boiling water is time-consuming and requires a heat source, making it impractical for large-scale implementation.

  1. Membrane Filtration

Membrane filtration is another purification method that employs a physical barrier to remove contaminants from water. Various types of membrane filtration, such as microfiltration, ultrafiltration, and nanofiltration, effectively eliminate both microbial and chemical pollutants. These methods are applicable for purifying water from diverse sources like lakes and rivers. However, membrane filtration can be expensive to implement and demands substantial energy consumption.

  1. UV Disinfection

UV disinfection is a quick and efficient water purification method that utilizes ultraviolet light to kill bacteria and viruses. While it does not eliminate chemical contaminants, UV disinfection is commonly used worldwide to effectively disinfect water. However, it may not be suitable for heavily contaminated water with chemical pollutants.

  1. Activated Carbon Absorption

Activated carbon absorption involves using activated carbon to remove organic and inorganic contaminants from water. With its high surface area and porosity, activated carbon traps and eliminates a wide range of pollutants, including pesticides, chlorine, and volatile organic compounds. However, activated carbon needs frequent replacement, leading to higher costs.

  1. Chemical Coagulation and Sedimentation

Chemical coagulation and sedimentation is a process that destabilizes and aggregates contaminants in water using chemicals. The aggregated particles can then be removed through settling or filtration. This method is effective against suspended solids, organic compounds, and some inorganic contaminants. However, it may not be as effective against microbial pollutants, and the chemicals used can have adverse environmental impacts if not properly disposed of.

Preparation & Sample Collection

  1. Gather the necessary equipment, including:
    • Sterilized containers for water sample collection
    • Labels for sample identification
    • Appropriate safety gear (gloves, goggles, etc.)
  2. Select two different polluted rivers or lakesides as sample sources.
  3. Using clean, sterilized containers, collect water samples of approximately 3000 ml from each location.
  4. Label each sample with the source and any relevant information.

Boiling

  • Bunsen burner
  • Heat-resistant glass beaker
  • Thermometer
  • Timer
  1. Pour the water sample (500 ml) into a clean heat-resistant glass beaker.
  2. Place the beaker on a Bunsen burner or stove and heat until the water reaches a rolling boil.
  3. Allow the water to boil vigorously for 10 minutes.
  4. Remove the beaker from the heat source and let it cool.
  5. Transfer a sample (100 ml) of the boiled water to a clean container for further analysis.

Membrane Filtration

  • Filtration system
    • Vacuum filtration apparatus
    • Membrane filtration unit
  • 0.2-micron pore size membrane filter
  • Filter holder & filter flask
  • Vacuum pump & pressure system
  1. Pour the water sample (500 ml) into a clean filtration flask.
  2. Attach the filtration flask to the filtration apparatus, ensuring a tight connection.
  3. Apply gentle suction or pressure to initiate the filtration process.
  4. Collect the filtered water in a clean container.
  5. Transfer a sample (100 ml) of the filtered water for further analysis.

UV Disinfection

  • UV lamp (wavelength: 254 nm)
  • Transparent quartz container (500 ml)
  • Power supply for the UV device
  • Timer
  1. Fill a transparent quartz container with the water sample, ensuring the entire volume is exposed to UV light.
  2. Place the container under a UV lamp.
  3. Allow the water to be irradiated for 30 minutes.
  4. Cover and store the disinfected water in a clean container.
  5. Transfer a sample (100 ml) of the disinfected water for further analysis.

Activated Carbon Absorption

  • Activated carbon cartridge
  • Activated carbon filter system
  • Filter holder
  1. Pour the water sample (500 ml) into a clean filtration flask.
  2. Attach the carbon filter cartridge to the filtration apparatus.
  3. Allow the water to pass through the filter slowly.
  4. Collect the filtered water in a clean container.
  5. Transfer a sample (100 ml) of the filtered water for further analysis.

Chemical Coagulation and Sedimentation

  • Ferric chloride (coagulant)
  • Mixing container
  • Magnetic stirrer
  • Settling container
  • Timer
  1. Pour the water sample (500 ml) into a clean glass beaker.
  2. Add the appropriate dosage of ferric chloride coagulant.
  3. Stir gently for 2 minutes to facilitate coagulation.
  4. Allow the water to settle undisturbed for 1 hour.
  5. Carefully decant the clarified water into a settling container, leaving behind the sediment.
  6. Transfer a sample (100 ml) of the clarified water for further analysis.

Water Quality Testing

  1. pH Measurement
    • pH meter
    • pH test strips
    • Calibrate the pH meter according to the manufacturer's instructions.
    • Immerse the pH electrode into the water sample and wait for the reading to stabilize.
    • Record the pH value or match the color of the test strip with the provided color chart to obtain the pH value.
  2. Turbidity Measurement (NTU)
    • Turbidimeter
    • Turbidity meter
    • Calibrate the turbidimeter according to the manufacturer's instructions.
    • Fill a clean cuvette with the water sample and insert it into the turbidimeter.
    • Record the turbidity reading in Nephelometric Turbidity Units (NTU) displayed on the device.
  3. Total Dissolved Solids Measurement (mg/L)
    • TDS meter
    • Conductivity meter
    • Calibrate the TDS meter according to the manufacturer's instructions.
    • Immerse the TDS probe into the water sample and allow it to stabilize.
    • Record the TDS reading displayed on the meter in milligrams per liter (mg/L) or parts per million (ppm).
  4. Pollutant Concentration Measurement (mg/L)
    • Spectrophotometer
    • Colourimeter
    • Reagents (specific to the pollutants)
    • Prepare the necessary reagents according to the test kits or methods used.
    • Follow the manufacturer's instructions to perform the specific tests for pollutants such as nitrates, phosphates, heavy metals, etc.
    • Measure the absorbance of the resulting solution using a spectrophotometer or colorimeter.
    • Use the calibration curve provided by the manufacturer to determine the pollutant concentration in milligrams per liter (mg/L).
  5. Bacterial Contaminant Measurement (CFU/mL)
    • Microbiological test kit
    • Petri dishes
    • Agar medium
    • Prepare the necessary agar medium according to the manufacturer's instructions.
    • Inoculate the agar plates with water samples using the pour plate or spread plate method.
    • Incubate the plates at the appropriate temperature and time specified by the test kit.
    • Count the number of colony-forming units (cfu) present on the agar plates and record the results in colony-forming units per milliliter (cfu/mL).
  6. Viral Contaminant Measurement (PFU/mL)
    • Viral test kit
    • Follow the instructions provided by the viral test kit to perform the viral contaminant measurement.
    • Collect the appropriate volume of water sample as per the test kit instructions.
    • Process the sample according to the viral test kit protocol.
    • Quantify the viral contaminant concentration in plaque-forming units per milliliter (pfu/mL) using the methods specified by the kit.

Individuals can play a crucial role in reducing water consumption and contributing to water conservation efforts alongside water purification. Firstly, practicing mindful water usage habits such as turning off taps when not in use, taking shorter showers, and fixing any leaks promptly can significantly reduce water waste.
